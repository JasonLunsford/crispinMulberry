SOW Highlighted Questions - Answered

Javascript Charting Engine

For this project we will use the d3.js engine. This engine offers fantastic visualization options (the Examples page is pretty cool) and a sensible interface for accessing data nodes. The list of reasons to choose d3.js is long. The engine allows the binding of arbitrary data to the DOM, and makes accessing nodes easy using a selector technique defined by the W3C Selectors API (it also supports the sizzle engine to support node selection within older browsers). It does all of that without using a proprietary framework, dealing strictly with HTML, CSS, and SVG.

Additionally, d3.js supports all of our obligated browsers, including IE9. It is also totally compatible with both AngularJS (check out Sky Blue Trades Radian project) and Node.js. As it turns out I am recommending both of these technologies for ABC, Inc’s Campaign Analytics Dashboard project.

Deployment Plan Methodology

Project Methodology: Agile

Agile will afford our team the ability to produce units of functional work at predictable intervals suitable for demonstration to stakeholders and other, non-technical team members committed to the project at large.

Agile will also help me as team lead create timelines and offer time-to-completion estimates without the overhead of downstream deadlines generated based on my estimates. This assumes both client and institutional commitment to the Agile philosophy. Long term estimates will be generated by the formation of epics (essentially, big stories with SWAG attached based on prior experience with similar work). Later, stories will be written by both the stakeholders and members of my team that will cover the completion of the epic. The relative difficulty of these stories will be measured by my team, information with which I use to further refine the time-to-delivery estimate of the particular epic.

Day to day, my team will operate within a sprint - typically about 2 weeks long, with an assumption of approximately 6 hours per day of work per team member. Sprint length and hours-per-day of actual work are but two of many attributes that will require fine tuning over time (outside the scope of this project) - the entire array of these attributes together form my team’s velocity, a relative measure of how productive my team is in general, and a major asset in estimating time-to-completion for projects going forward.

For Agile to work in a reliable and productive fashion all members of the project, client, institution, and technical members must commit to the unique constraints, requirements, and workflow of the process. Multiple meetings will be required, some involving my team members, others involving only the stakeholders and team leads. Daily meetings are necessary within my team in order to help keep parity between members. Meetings to discuss story priority, estimate story difficulty, pick stories / write tasks for the story, and discuss how a sprint was handled (held at the end of the sprint) are examples of more meetings necessary under Agile. Finally, it will be my responsibility to prepare a presentation to the stakeholders every two weeks. This presentation will show the completed units of work, thereby affirming the project is moving in a regular, timely fashion. This is also the time to answer any questions generated by the presentation. Often, demonstrations are a catalyst for clients to speak up (typically after the meeting!) when the project is not going in a satisfactory way. Consequently epics can be re-evaluated and stories refashioned, allowing for mid-stream direction changes that can often keep the project on track. Ultimately, this is the power of Agile!

Assumptions

Artwork & Provided Files

We assume the client will provide all necessary wireframes for the global project (Digital Nervous System) as well as workflow-centric wireframes for the specific Health Monitor project.

We assume a branding style guide will be furnished, complete with (but not limited to) color palette, iconography, typography, and button / anchor styling.

Finally, we assume all necessary images, such as company logo, will be furnished in whatever image quality the client prefers.

Technology Requirements

The client has full access to their hosted environments, such that the installation of server side languages like Node.js is possible and regular access to production logs - via automated scripting most likely - won’t be a problem.

Client will provide Nerdy staff access to those hosted environments for the duration of the installation period - with at least one account given admin privileges for the trouble free installation of Node.js.

The Nerdery will employ cache busting techniques whenever the benefits of caching are outweighed by the risk of displaying stale client data - and that these techniques will be refined during the course of development if performance vs data relevance becomes an issue.

Deployment

The Nerdery will install Node.js, the selected Node.js MVC framework, build the folder structure used in The Nerdery Production environment, re-configure any API calls / URL paths necessary to adapt to the new hosted environment, and verify the site comes up for an ABC, Inc employee from within a network where the Dashboard will be used.

Following deployment, The Nerdery will work with ABC, Inc staff to transfer Regression tests to ABC, Inc testing servers and fully train their QA engineers on using them. If a test (or tests) fail to produce green lights The Nerdery staff will troubleshoot and repair misconfigured code.

Future Project Considerations

Once the client shares our code base we should consider building a shared git environment. The benefit here would be that we could configure ABC, Inc’s production environment to parallel our own - and therefore development workflow could progress from The Nerdery environments directly into client’s production environment, cutting down deployment complexity following the release of new projects.
